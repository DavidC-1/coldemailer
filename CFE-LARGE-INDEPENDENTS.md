# Roland Frasier CFE — Deal Structures for Large Independent Self-Storage Operators (Non-REIT, Non-Mom-and-Pop)

Source: Synthesis of Roland Frasier's EPIC Network materials (`knowledge-base/06-roland-frasier/`), the Radius+ 2026 Forecast (Pinecone `self-storage-reports` index), and the operator-language conventions established in [`CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md`](./CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md). This is the **large-independent companion** to that file: where `CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md` covers single-facility and 2–3 facility owners, this file covers the regional/independent operator that sits between a single-owner mom-and-pop and an institutional REIT.

**Target sizing for this playbook:** ~5–50 facilities, professional staff, formal (often audited or reviewed) financials, CFO and/or board or family ownership group, EBITDA roughly **$2M–$30M**, enterprise value roughly **$20M–$300M**.

**Why this segment is different from a mom-and-pop:**
- Owners are sophisticated. They've already been pitched by REIT M&A teams, brokers, and PE platforms. Cold "wallet-vs-pipe-wrench" gambits fall flat.
- Books are clean (or close). You'll see audited or reviewed financials, real working-capital schedules, real diligence.
- There is almost always a moat-shaped gap: ownership is tired or undercapitalized vs. the opportunity, but the business is too big to be cheap and too small to attract a REIT bid.
- This is exactly where Roland's **Consulting → Equity → Option to Acquire** playbook is most effective.

**Why this segment is different from the small operators in `CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md`:**
- Bigger absolute dollars. A $5K/month retainer signals junior. Diagnostic and advisory fees are 5–10× what they are at single-facility scale.
- Real capital stack. Senior debt, mezz, PPM, RE SLB, ESOP — these are live tools at this size, not theoretical.
- Board/CFO buyer, not founder buyer. The pitch goes through a finance lens, not just an operator lens.
- Real estate engineering matters more. On asset-heavy independent self-storage, OpCo/PropCo splits and sale-leaseback structures often hide more value than the operating lift.

---

## 1. Target Profile — Who Fits This Playbook

A large independent self-storage business worth approaching with CFE-style structures typically shows:

- **Owner-operator fatigue at scale** — founder is still CEO, business has outgrown their operating model
- **Stalled growth despite a strong base** — revenue flat or single-digit growth for 2–3 years
- **Sub-institutional sophistication** — no PE owner, no banker on retainer, no permanent CFO
- **Capital constraint** — refuses to take outside money, can't fund the next expansion themselves
- **Succession ambiguity** — no clear internal successor; kids aren't taking it; key #2 is restless
- **Maturing low-coupon debt** — 2019–2021 loans coming due into a much-higher-rate market
- **Underexploited dormant assets** — customer list, real estate, brand, supplier relationships, data, unused acreage, geographic footprint
- **Exit interest within 2–5 years** but no plan for how

For self-storage specifically: regional operators with **10–40 facilities**, mixed owned/leased, often a holding-company structure, frequently family-owned 2nd generation. Per the Radius+ 2026 Forecast there are roughly **6,500 large private companies** and **9,000 public-company facilities** vs **43,500 small private companies** — the large-independent segment is where deal-making pencil is widest and where REIT competition is thinnest.

These owners will *not* sell at REIT cap rates to you on a cash deal — but they will entertain a structured deal that protects the price they want.

---

## 2. Why CFE Beats a Cash Offer at This Level

Mid-market independent owners reject lowball cash bids on reflex. CFE flips the conversation:

1. **You're not negotiating price first** — you're solving their growth or succession problem.
2. **You're paid to learn the business** before you commit capital.
3. **You lock optionality** (purchase option) at today's price *before* you create tomorrow's value.
4. **Equity bridges the gap** between what they want and what the market will pay.

The 2026 Radius+ data makes this explicit. Tyler Lambert (Live Oak Bank): *"We are seeing deals fall apart because buyers cannot bridge the gap between seller pricing expectations and today's debt costs."* Michael Mazur (Newcoast): *"Everyone is waiting for the reset. Sellers still want peak pricing, and buyers cannot get there with current debt."* This is precisely the gap a CFE-with-option structure was designed to bridge — seller gets their number, buyer doesn't overpay because the option price is locked before the value is created.

The Roland framing: *"Exit outcomes are usually decided long before a deal is on the table."* For larger sellers, you are positioning to be the only credible buyer at the seller's number, two years before they go to market.

## 3. The Core Sequence — "Consulting Into Equity Into Acquisition"

Adapted from Roland's "Evil Plan." At mid-market scale, each step is more formal and more expensive.

### Step 1 — Paid Diagnostic
- **Not free** at this level. Free advice signals you're junior. Charge.
- Roland's rates as anchor: **$18K–$20K half-day virtual**, **$50K/quarter** advisory, **$150K/6 months** half-day monthly.
- The diagnostic produces a written **Value Creation Plan**: 3–5 specific initiatives that close the gap between today's value and the seller's price target.
- Output is *theirs to keep* — they're free to walk and run the plan themselves. Almost no mid-market owner does.

### Step 2 — F2E Engagement
- Convert to a **Fee-to-Equity** structure: monthly retainer plus equity component.
- Equity range at this scale: **5%–20%** for active operating involvement (Type 2: Operating), or **0.25%–5%** for advisory-only (Type 1).
- Use **"Greater Of"** language: monthly cash floor *or* % of distributions, whichever is greater.
- Vesting: **Instant Earn-In** is the ask. Fall back to **Temporal Milestone** (at least 20% up front, no less than 50%, rest vesting over time, full acceleration on termination without cause).
- Always include the **business pre-nup**: pre-negotiated buyout formula (e.g., "40% of 4× 12-month trailing SDE paid over 18 months") so divorce terms are settled while you're friends.

### Step 3 — Lock the Purchase Option
- **Full-Option to Acquire** (Strategy #132) or **Partial-Option** (#131) embedded in the same agreement.
- Two-year option window standard. Three years if they're hesitant.
- **Price is fixed today** — before the value you're about to create gets baked in.
- Frame it as alignment: *"If I'm going to put my reputation and time into this, I need the option to buy you out within two years. If the plan doesn't work, the option expires worthless and you've lost nothing."*

### Step 4 — Create the Value
- Execute the plan. Roland's expectation: 12–24 months of measurable growth before exercising.
- Throughout, you're inside the books — best possible DD environment.
- This is also where you stack the deal-funding strategies (Section 5) to make the eventual close cash-light.

### Step 5 — Exercise the Option, Stack Strategies to Fund the Close
- Exercise at the locked-in price.
- The business is worth materially more than the strike price. The delta is your equity-financed "discount."
- Apply the **216 Deal Funding Strategies** to fund the actual purchase with as little personal cash as possible.

## 4. The Economic Framework

Three numbers anchor every deal at this scale:

- **Baseline EBITDA snapshot** — frozen at engagement, audited or reviewed by a mutually-agreed third party (often Quality-of-Earnings firm).
- **Stabilized cap rate for the market** — typically 6.0–7.0% for institutional-quality self-storage in 2026 (lower for top-tier markets, higher for tertiary).
- **Value created** = (EBITDA lift × 1/cap) + any multiple-arbitrage from platform building.

**Rule of thumb:** your total compensation (fees + equity-on-paper) should be **15–25% of the value you create**, not a fixed dollar figure. Larger deals get a lower take-rate; sophisticated owners run the math.

**Worked example:** Lift EBITDA from $8M to $11M on a 6.5% cap → $46M of value created. Your target take across fees + equity ≈ $7M–$11M over 24–36 months.

**Personal filter:** Only do deals where modeled equity value at year 3–5 is ≥ $1M. At this segment, the floor is higher than at the small-operator level because the engagement cost (your time, your team, legal, QoE) is higher.

## 5. Standard Term-Sheet Elements (Apply to All Deals Below)

Every deal at this scale layers on top of this skeleton. Individual structures vary the fee/equity mix — these terms are constant.

- **Baseline establishment:** Trailing-12 audited or reviewed P&L, rent roll snapshot, achieved-rate vs web-rate audit, ECRI history, ancillary attach rates, delinquency aging, captured-call audit, real-estate schedule with ownership/lease structure. All numbers signed off before any work begins.
- **Term:** 36 months default for F2E. 60 months for equity-heavy or roll-up structures.
- **Governance:** Board seat or board-observer right on F2E and larger. Information rights: monthly P&L, quarterly bank statements, annual audited financials, annual third-party valuation.
- **Vesting:** Equity vests over the term with a 12-month cliff (or Instant Earn-In if owner accepts). Acceleration on change-of-control, refi above threshold, or termination without cause.
- **IP ownership:** You retain ownership of any AI agents, codebases, prompts, models, SOPs you build. Operator gets a perpetual license for use at their facilities during the term. License terminates if equity is wound down for cause.
- **Business pre-nup:** Pre-negotiated buyout formula in every operating agreement. The formula must work even if the relationship sours. Roland's standard language: "40% of 4× trailing-12 SDE paid over 18 months" — adjust to scale.
- **Drag-along, tag-along, ROFR.** Non-negotiable. Pre-negotiate the trigger thresholds (typically a sale of >50% of equity, or any change-of-control event).
- **Non-compete / non-circumvent:** Bilateral. You can't pursue competing facilities in their MSA during the term; they can't retain your methods, vendors, or staff outside the agreement.
- **Performance kicker definition:** Always defined as *delta above baseline trailing-12 EBITDA*, never gross dollars. Audited annually by mutually-agreed QoE firm.
- **Working capital peg:** Negotiated at the LOI stage. At this scale a wrong peg costs millions at close.
- **Real estate carve:** Owned real estate is always separately disclosed and valued. The option price specifies whether it's an OpCo-only or OpCo+PropCo strike.
- **Termination for cause:** 90 days notice for material breach; equity treatment specified per deal type. Wrongful termination accelerates 100% of vesting.
- **PCI / tenant-data compliance:** Voice/chat agents handling payment data routed through PCI-DSS-compliant infrastructure; DPA in place before agents go live.

### Deal #1 — Paid Diagnostic + Value Creation Plan (The Door Opener)

**Who it's for:** Every large-independent target. Always lead with this. It's the equivalent of the "Front Door" deal in `CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md` but at 10× the dollar level and with a written, board-presentable deliverable.

**The pitch (one line):** "Pay me a fixed fee for a structured diagnostic of your business. I'll deliver a written Value Creation Plan in 60 days — three to five initiatives, sized in dollars, that close the gap between what your business is worth today and what you want for it. The plan is yours to keep, with or without me."

**Deliverables:**
- 60-day engagement, 8–12 facility walks (if regional), 15–25 stakeholder interviews (owner, CFO, key managers, top customers if accessible)
- Quality-of-Earnings-style review of trailing-24-month P&L with normalization
- Achieved-rate vs web-rate audit across the portfolio (Radius+ data shows REITs are widening this gap aggressively — independents are usually under-pricing)
- ECRI / rate-increase discipline audit
- Captured-call and lead-conversion audit (install call tracking week 1)
- Ancillary attach audit (insurance, retail, late fees, admin fees)
- Delinquency aging review
- OpCo/PropCo structural analysis: which dirt is owned, which is leased, what the lease terms look like, what a sale-leaseback unlock would produce
- Competitive intel report on the operator's trade areas using Radius+ data
- Written 30–60 page **Value Creation Plan** with 3–5 sized initiatives, dollarized to enterprise-value lift

**Fee mechanics:**
- **Flat fee $50K–$150K** depending on portfolio size:
  - 5–10 facilities: $50K
  - 10–25 facilities: $100K
  - 25+ facilities: $150K
- Half due at signing, half due on delivery.
- Optional: discount of 50% of the fee credited toward Deal #3 (F2E) if owner converts within 90 days.

**Equity mechanics:**
- None. This is a clean consulting engagement.
- The intent is to *not* take equity here — the conversion to F2E in Deal #3 is where equity enters.

**Success metrics:**
- Plan delivered on time, signed off by owner and (if applicable) board.
- Plan contains ≥ 3 initiatives sized at ≥ 5% EBITDA lift each.
- Owner converts to F2E (Deal #3) within 90 days. Conversion rate target: 40–60%.

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- Engagement is fixed-fee, fixed-scope, fixed-term. There is no failure mode. Even if owner walks, you've collected a real fee and gotten paid to learn the business.

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "Why would I pay you to tell me how to run my own business?" → "Because you already know how to run it. You hired me because you can't see what's invisible from the inside — and because the version of this business that's worth 30% more in 24 months is going to look different from today's version. I'm paid to draw that picture."
- "$100K is a lot for a written report." → "It's about 2% of the value the plan typically identifies. If I find $5M of EV lift, the report paid for itself 50×."
- "What if I run the plan myself?" → "You can. The plan is yours. Most owners discover that running the plan is harder than writing it, and that's where Deal #3 picks up."

### Deal #2 — Type 1 Advisory CFE (Board Seat + Light Equity)

**Who it's for:** Family-owned multi-generational businesses where the owner wants outside perspective, generational transition help, and network access — but isn't ready to hand over operations. Often the wedge into a larger deal later.

**The pitch (one line):** "I take a board seat, attend 4 meetings a year, give you direct access to my network for partnerships, capital, and deal flow, and challenge your strategic thinking. You pay a quarterly retainer plus give me a small equity stake to make sure I show up like an owner."

**Deliverables:**
- Board seat or board-observer position
- 4 board meetings per year + 6 strategic check-ins
- Quarterly written strategic memo
- Network introductions: capital providers, brokers, M&A bankers, vendor consolidation partners, tech partners
- Strategic-question facilitation for ownership group (especially valuable for family businesses with generational transition tension)
- Annual strategic offsite facilitation (optional, billable separately)
- M&A pipeline curation if owner is interested in tuck-in acquisitions

**Fee mechanics:**
- **$50K/quarter retainer** (mirrors Roland's anchor rate). 24-month minimum term, then quarter-to-quarter.
- Annual strategic offsite: $25K flat (if used).
- Travel and 3rd-party expenses passed through at cost.

**Equity mechanics:**
- **0.25%–5% common equity** in the operating company.
- Sizing rule of thumb: 0.25%–1% for portfolios > $50M EV; 2–5% for portfolios $10M–$50M EV.
- Vesting: Temporal Milestone — 33% at signing, 33% at month 12, 34% at month 24. Full acceleration on change-of-control or wrongful termination.
- Liquidity on: sale, refi above 75% LTV release threshold, recap, or year-5 buyout right at trailing-12 EBITDA × market multiple.
- ROFR, tag-along, drag-along. Pre-nup buyout formula.

**Success metrics:**
- Owner-Net-Promoter ≥ 9 at annual review.
- At least 1 material strategic decision per year informed by your work (acquisition, divestiture, capital event, partnership, succession plan).
- Quantifiable outcome: ≥ 2 portfolio improvements per year, sized in dollars.

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- Either party can terminate at year 2 anniversary with 90 days notice. Vested equity remains.
- For-cause termination by owner: unvested equity forfeit; vested remains.
- Wrongful termination: full acceleration.

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "We've never had a board." → "That's exactly the point. The businesses that command institutional multiples have boards. Two years from now if you want a strategic exit, the buyer is going to ask who's on your board. The answer 'no one' is a discount; the answer 'an industry advisor with a track record' is a premium."
- "Why equity? Just charge me more." → "Because I want to show up like an owner, not a vendor. The retainer is for my time. The equity is for my alignment."

### Deal #3 — Type 2 Operating CFE / F2E (The Core "Evil Plan")

**Who it's for:** The mid-market operator who is tired, growth has stalled, and they need an executor — not just an advisor. Most common conversion from Deal #1.

**The pitch (one line):** "I become your operating partner for the next 24–36 months. I run the Value Creation Plan you already paid for. You pay me a monthly fee plus equity, and we share in the lift. At the end of the term, you have a materially more valuable business — and we've pre-agreed on what happens next, whether that's a sale, a recap, or a continued partnership."

**Deliverables:**
- Co-execution of the Value Creation Plan from Deal #1 (or new Plan if engaged directly)
- Embedded operating role: COO-equivalent or strategic-projects lead, 4–6 days/month on-site, daily availability
- Portfolio-wide AI-agent stack install (voice, chat, ECRI orchestration, insurance attach, delinquency, document processing — same toolkit as `CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md` but at portfolio scale)
- Centralized revenue management discipline. Per Radius+ ch. 8: *"Scaled operators have developed revenue management systems capable of analyzing local conditions, seasonality, competitor pricing, and demand profiles in real time. Smaller operators often lack these tools."* This is the single biggest EBITDA lever at the large-independent scale.
- Centralized marketing and customer-acquisition cost (CAC) reduction
- Group purchasing on insurance, tax appeal, vendor consolidation
- Quarterly board reporting
- M&A pipeline if growth-by-acquisition is on the plan
- Exit preparation: QoE-ready financials, normalized add-backs, deal book, banker selection (months 18–24)

**Fee mechanics:**
- **Base fee tier by portfolio size:**

| Tier | Portfolio EBITDA | Monthly Fee | Performance Kicker | Equity |
|------|------------------|-------------|--------------------|--------|
| L1   | $2M–$5M          | $25K/mo     | 20% of EBITDA lift | 10%–15% |
| L2   | $5M–$15M         | $40K/mo     | 20% of EBITDA lift | 8%–12%  |
| L3   | $15M+            | $60K/mo     | 25% of EBITDA lift | 5%–10%  |

- "Greater Of" language: monthly cash floor *or* % of distributions, whichever is greater.
- Performance kicker calculated annually against audited delta-EBITDA, paid within 60 days of QoE audit.
- Base fee continues for term length; performance kicker has a 36-month tail post-term.

**Equity mechanics:**
- Common units (or LP units if HoldCo is structured as LP/GP).
- Vesting: **Instant Earn-In** is the ask. Fallback: Temporal Milestone with no less than 20% up front, 50% target up front, remainder over 24 months, full acceleration on change-of-control or wrongful termination.
- Liquidity on: sale, refi above 75% LTV release threshold, recap, year-5 buyout right at trailing-12 EBITDA × market multiple.
- Pre-nup: business buyout formula (40% of 4× trailing-12 SDE paid over 18 months, scaled to deal size).
- ROFR, tag-along, drag-along.

**Success metrics:**
- EBITDA delta ≥ 15% by month 18.
- EBITDA delta ≥ 25% by month 36.
- Achieved-rate to web-rate spread closed by ≥ 50%.
- ECRI cycle executed on full book within 6 months.
- Insurance attach ≥ 60% portfolio-wide by month 12.
- Delinquency 30+ aging reduced ≥ 30% by month 12.
- Portfolio-wide captured-call rate ≥ 95% by month 6.

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- If 18-month EBITDA delta < 8%: operator can terminate at month 18 with 90 days notice; equity vested-to-date holds, no further vesting.
- If you exit for cause: full vesting accelerates.
- Wrongful termination: 100% acceleration + buyout under pre-nup formula.

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "10% equity is a lot." → "It's 10% of the upside we create together. Your downside is a $40K/month fee and the Value Creation Plan we already agreed to. If we don't lift EBITDA, you keep your business and we walk away with what we vested for the work we already did. If we do lift it 25%, you keep 90% of a much bigger pie."
- "What if I sell in year 2?" → "Then your sale price reflects our lift, you get 90% of it, and I get 10% of it. We win when you win — and we've designed the deal so a fast exit is fine."
- "Why isn't this a consulting fee?" → "Because consultants leave. Equity holders push for the right outcome — including pushing back on you when it matters."

### Deal #4 — Performance-Segment Earn-In (Carve a Growth Lane)

**Who it's for:** Mid-market operators who have one specific growth initiative (open 5 new facilities, integrate a recent acquisition, launch a new line of business such as boat/RV storage, expand into a new MSA) — but don't want to give up equity in the whole business.

**The pitch (one line):** "I take equity only in the new initiative — not in your existing business. I run the growth play, you keep your core business unchanged, and we split the upside on what we build together."

**Deliverables:**
- Dedicated SPV or class of equity for the specific initiative
- Project leadership: site selection, capital raise, construction oversight, lease-up, or acquisition integration depending on the segment
- Full AI-agent stack install at any new facilities the initiative produces
- Pipeline / pre-development management if the initiative is greenfield expansion
- Quarterly steering committee reporting

**Fee mechanics:**
- $20K–$40K/mo segment retainer (scaled to segment scope)
- 1.5% acquisition fee on any deals closed within the initiative
- 1.0% development fee on any greenfield project costs the initiative completes
- Performance kicker: 25% of segment-level EBITDA above pro-forma underwriting, paid annually

**Equity mechanics:**
- **5%–25% equity in the segment SPV** (not the core business):
  - 5–10% for low-risk, capital-rich initiatives where you're mainly the operator
  - 15–20% for mixed risk initiatives where you're sourcing + executing
  - 20–25% for high-risk initiatives where you're putting reputation and time at risk
- **Performance Segment Earn-In** structure: failure of the *core* business doesn't poison your segment stake; failure of the *segment* doesn't poison the core.
- Vesting: 50% at SPV closing or first deal close, 50% over 36 months.
- ROFR on segment-level exits.

**Success metrics:**
- Segment EBITDA ≥ underwriting model within 24 months
- Segment-level NOI ≥ debt service + reserve by month 18 if leveraged
- Number of facilities / units / segment lines completed per plan

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- Segment SPV exits independently of core business. You can be sold out, drag-along into a portfolio sale, or hold to a year-7 buyout right.
- If segment underperforms model by > 25% at month 24: operator has buyout right at vested-equity book value.

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "Why not just charge for the project work?" → "Because I'm taking development and execution risk with you. Project fees pay me for time. Equity pays me for outcomes. You only pay equity if the outcome happens."
- "What if you cherry-pick the easy segment?" → "I don't pick the segment — you do. You've already decided this is the growth lane. My equity is sized to the risk profile of that lane."

### Deal #5 — F2E + Full-Option to Acquire (The Locked Price)

**Who it's for:** Mid-market owners with a 2–5 year exit interest but no plan, no banker, and no buyer at their price. This is Roland's canonical "Evil Plan" applied at scale and is the single highest-leverage structure in the playbook.

**The pitch (one line):** "I become your operating partner for 24 months. I lift EBITDA. And in exchange for putting my time and reputation into this, I get the right to buy you out within 24 months at a price we agree on today, before the lift we're about to create. If the lift doesn't happen, the option expires worthless. You've lost nothing. If it does happen, I'm the buyer who can close at your number — and you don't have to go through a sale process."

**Deliverables:**
- All Deal #3 (F2E) deliverables
- Embedded **Full Option to Acquire** (Roland strategy #132): right but not obligation to purchase the entire business at a fixed strike, exercisable any time within 24 months (extendable to 36 if owner is hesitant)
- Quarterly option-mark report so owner sees what the option is worth as EBITDA lifts
- Exit-prep work in parallel (QoE, deal book, transferability audit) so close can happen in 60 days if exercised

**Fee mechanics:**
- Same as Deal #3 (Tier L1/L2/L3 base + 20–25% performance kicker)
- 50% credit of base fees-paid against any exercised option purchase price (operator's incentive to let you exercise)

**Equity mechanics:**
- 5%–10% baseline equity in the operating company (lower than Deal #3 because the option is the real value)
- Vesting: same as Deal #3
- ROFR, tag-along, drag-along

**Option mechanics (the heart of this deal):**
- **Strike price = fixed formula:** e.g., "6.5× trailing-12 EBITDA as of execution date" or "$X flat dollars." Never "fair market value at exercise" — that defeats the entire purpose.
- Option exercisable any time within 24 months (or 36 if needed to close the deal)
- **Kill fee if owner sells to a third party during the option period:** typically 3–5% of the third-party transaction value. This is what locks the owner in.
- Right of First Refusal layered on top of the option, so if a third-party bid lands above the strike, you can match.
- Real-estate carve specified: option price includes OpCo + PropCo, or OpCo only, with real estate at a market lease — pre-negotiated.
- Standard purchase agreement form attached to option document (so exercise is a 60-day close, not a negotiation).

**Success metrics:**
- EBITDA delta ≥ 20% by month 18 (so the option is comfortably in the money)
- Option exercised, OR option price exceeded by a third-party bid (you get the kill fee), OR option allowed to expire with full fee retention.

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- If you exercise: 60-day close at the agreed strike. Funding via the Section 5 stack (carryback + RE SLB + PPM + senior + mezz).
- If owner sells to third party: kill fee due at the third-party close.
- If you don't exercise by month 24: option expires worthless. All prior fees retained, equity vesting continues per Deal #3 terms.

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "Why would I give you a right to buy me at today's price?" → "Because you don't have a buyer at today's price. The market gap is real — Live Oak Bank's data confirms it. I'm offering to be the buyer who shows up two years from now at your number, when no one else will. The price you're locking in is a price you can't get from anyone else today."
- "What if I want to sell at year 1 to a REIT?" → "You can. ROFR means you bring me the bid first. If I match, I close. If I don't, you sell and I get the kill fee. Either way, you got 12+ months of EBITDA lift, you've got a banker-ready deal book, and the REIT pays you more than they would have a year ago."

### Deal #6 — F2E + Partial-Option to Acquire (Buy 51% Later)

**Who it's for:** Owners who explicitly want to stay involved post-exit (continued board seat, ongoing partial economic interest, family legacy reasons) or owners who want to de-risk by selling control but keeping skin in the game.

**The pitch (one line):** "Same as Deal #5, but the option is for control — not the whole thing. I buy 51% at year 2, you keep 49%. You take chips off the table, you stay involved as we keep growing, and we have a pre-agreed put-call schedule for the remainder."

**Deliverables:**
- Same as Deal #5

**Fee mechanics:**
- Same as Deal #5

**Equity mechanics:**
- Same as Deal #5

**Option mechanics:**
- **Strike formula for 51%:** e.g., "6.5× trailing-12 EBITDA × 51%, with control premium adder of 10%."
- 24-month option window, kill fee, ROFR — same as Deal #5
- **Put-call ladder for remaining 49%:**
  - Owner has *put* right on remaining 49% any time year 4–7 at then-trailing-12 EBITDA × pre-agreed multiple
  - You have *call* right on remaining 49% at year 7 at the same multiple
  - Multiple specified at signing: e.g., 7.0× (premium over the original 6.5× control strike, to share the value lift you create together post-control)
- Post-control governance: you control the board, owner retains 1 board seat, key vetoes on related-party transactions
- Continued distributions to owner per ownership percentage; pref returns layered on first if owner contributed working capital

**Success metrics:**
- Same as Deal #5 through month 18
- Post-control: continued EBITDA growth to support put-call multiples
- Smooth governance transition (measured by owner satisfaction and key-employee retention)

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- Same as Deal #5 for initial exercise
- Put-call ladder is contractual; either party can trigger per schedule
- If owner dies or becomes incapacitated during put-call period: ROFR to you at then-formula price; family bound to deal terms

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "I'm not selling 51% — that means you control it." → "Yes. That's the point. You'll be 67 years old when we trigger. You don't want to be running the board fights. You'll have control over your equity, your distributions, and your legacy — I'll have control over the operations. We've designed the governance to keep you in the meaningful seats."
- "Why not just sell 100%?" → "If 100% sale at your number is what you want, take Deal #5. Deal #6 is for owners who want to capture half the upside between year 2 and year 7 — and that's typically the highest-value half."

### Deal #7 — Acquisition Option with Kill Fee (Cold-Approach Option)

**Who it's for:** Targets you've identified but who aren't engaged on a F2E. They're not ready to give you equity or operations — but they're open to *locking a future buyer* in exchange for a fee and a price floor. Often used after an unsuccessful Deal #1 (Diagnostic) sale.

**The pitch (one line):** "Let's not do consulting. Let me just lock the right to buy you in 24 months at a price we agree on now. You keep running your business unchanged. I do exit-prep work in the background. If you sell to someone else, you pay me a kill fee. If I exercise, you get a clean exit at the price we shook hands on."

**Deliverables:**
- Quarterly QoE-style review (non-intrusive)
- Exit-readiness work in parallel: cleaning records, organizing real-estate documentation, normalizing financials, banker readiness
- Quarterly market intel report (Radius+ data + your acquisition pipeline)
- Standing exit-prep — owner is exit-ready at any time

**Fee mechanics:**
- $10K–$25K/quarter retainer (smaller than Deal #2 because scope is narrower)
- No performance kicker
- No equity

**Option mechanics:**
- Strike: 6.0×–7.0× trailing-12 EBITDA at execution date, fixed
- 24-month option window
- **Kill fee: 3–5% of any third-party sale price** during the option period
- ROFR on any third-party offer

**Success metrics:**
- Option exercised, OR third-party sale at higher price (you get kill fee), OR option expires with all fees retained
- Quarterly exit-prep deliverables completed on schedule

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- If exercise: 60-day close at agreed strike, funded via Section 5 stack
- If kill: kill fee due at third-party close
- If expiration: clean break, no further obligations either side

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "Why would I lock myself in?" → "You're not. You can sell to anyone you want. The option gives me a right; it doesn't take away yours. The kill fee is what makes the right meaningful — and the kill fee is small enough that any decent third-party bid covers it many times over."
- "What if my facility is worth more in 2 years?" → "Then we both win. You bring in a higher multiple in the open market, I get my kill fee, and you walk away with more money than you would have today."

### Section C — Capital Solutions for Owners Who Won't Sell

These deals are designed for owners whose constraint is *capital structure*, not *operating capability*. They don't want to give up the business — they want to fix the capital problem and keep operating.

### Deal #8 — OpCo / PropCo Sale-Leaseback Recap (The Self-Storage Lever)

**Who it's for:** Asset-heavy operators with substantial owned real estate (typical of large independents — often 60%+ of facilities are owned, not leased) who need capital for growth or recap and don't want to dilute or sell.

**The pitch (one line):** "Your facilities are worth more as separated entities. We split the OpCo from the PropCo, sell the real estate to a long-hold investor at a low cap rate, lease it back to you on a long-term net lease, and you redeploy the proceeds — into growth, into a partial cash-out for the family, or into a refi. I structure the transaction, run the OpCo, and take a piece of the OpCo equity for sourcing the deal."

**Deliverables:**
- Real estate appraisal (BOV) and structuring analysis on each facility
- PropCo investor sourcing from your network (REIT, family office, dedicated NNN buyer, 1031 buyer)
- Lease negotiation (long-term, 15-25 yr, NNN, with rent escalators that protect both sides)
- Newco formation: OpCo as a separate legal entity from PropCo
- Tax-deferred structuring where possible (1031, Opportunity Zone where applicable, partial cash-out via LLC distribution)
- Ongoing OpCo management or oversight per operator preference

**Fee mechanics:**
- 1.5% transaction fee on PropCo sale (paid at PropCo close)
- $40K–$60K structuring fee (paid at deal initiation, credited against transaction fee)
- Optional ongoing OpCo management fee: 3–5% of OpCo revenue (only if you're running OpCo post-deal)

**Equity mechanics:**
- **10%–20% common equity in OpCo** (the operating business post-separation)
- Vesting: 50% at PropCo close, 50% over 36 months
- No equity in PropCo (the dirt) — that's the owner's family wealth or the new PropCo investor's
- Standard ROFR, tag, drag on OpCo

**Strategy-stack references:**
- This is Roland Strategy **#148 (Real Estate Sale & Leaseback)** + **#1 (Asset Carve-Out)** + (optional) **#107 (Real Estate Split-Off)** if the family keeps the PropCo

**Success metrics:**
- PropCo transaction closes within 180 days at ≥ 90% of BOV
- OpCo achieves stabilized DSCR ≥ 1.30× post-lease within 12 months
- Family liquidity event achieved at owner's targeted dollar number

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- If PropCo transaction can't close at acceptable cap rate within 180 days: structuring fee retained, no further fees due, you can re-pitch as Deal #3 or Deal #5
- OpCo equity follows standard liquidity terms

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "I don't want to lease back my own facility — what if rent goes up?" → "Rent is contractually set with caps. Typical structure: 2% annual bumps with a CPI floor and a cap. Over 15 years, the lease costs you maybe 20% more than today. The cash you get at close compounds in growth or in family wealth — that math wins almost every time."
- "Why does the OpCo need you?" → "Because the OpCo without the real estate is a thinner business. It needs disciplined operations to support the lease. That's where I come in."

### Deal #9 — Real Estate Split-Off (Family Keeps the Dirt)

**Who it's for:** Family-owned operators where the *land* is the family wealth and the *operation* is the headache. Multi-generational owners who want to keep the dirt forever but stop running the storage business.

**The pitch (one line):** "Your family keeps the real estate as a long-term inheritance asset. I buy the operating business and lease back from the family at fair market rent. You become a passive landlord to me, you keep the long-term appreciation on the dirt, and I take operating risk off your hands."

**Deliverables:**
- Same as Deal #8, but PropCo stays with the family rather than being sold to a third party
- Family LLC structuring for the PropCo (estate planning, generational transfer, dynasty trust if applicable — coordinate with family's estate attorney)
- OpCo acquisition by you, funded via Section 5 stack
- Long-term NNN lease from family-PropCo to OpCo-you

**Fee mechanics:**
- $60K structuring fee (paid at signing)
- No transaction fee (no third-party PropCo buyer to fee)
- OpCo acquisition closing funded per Section 5

**Equity mechanics:**
- You own 100% of OpCo (or 100% less any management partner equity)
- Family owns 100% of PropCo
- No cross-equity

**Strategy-stack references:**
- Roland **Strategy #107 (Real Estate Split-Off)** + OpCo LBO using Strategies **#82–87 (seller carryback on OpCo), #119 (LBO), #122 (PPM), #199 (Mezzanine)**

**Success metrics:**
- Deal closes within 180 days
- OpCo achieves DSCR ≥ 1.30× post-acquisition within 18 months
- Family rent reliably paid, lease in good standing

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- OpCo exit independent of PropCo
- PropCo lease has standard institutional NNN terms (renewal options, rent reset mechanics, etc.)

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "Why would I sell the OpCo and keep the dirt? Isn't the operating business where the value is?" → "Today, the operating business is what's stressing you out. The dirt is the long-term family wealth. By splitting them, you let your kids and grandkids own a passive landlord position that grows with the market — and you let a professional buyer (me) take the operating risk. Most family wealth in this industry was built by people who held the dirt, not by people who ran the storage."

### Deal #10 — Sell & Stock Back with Promote (Family Stays In)

**Who it's for:** Family-owned exits where heirs want to stay in but the patriarch/matriarch wants to retire. Heirs aren't ready to run it, but they're ready to own a piece.

**The pitch (one line):** "You sell the business to my Newco. You take back minority stock — typically 20–30% — at the same valuation. I run the business. Your family stays in for the upside. We've pre-agreed on a put/call schedule for when family wants to fully exit."

**Deliverables:**
- Newco formation, transaction structuring
- Acquisition close, fully funded via Section 5 stack
- Family retains minority equity in Newco
- Governance structure: one family board seat, key vetoes on related-party transactions
- Ongoing distributions per Newco ownership and pref structure

**Fee mechanics:**
- 1.5% Newco transaction fee at close
- Ongoing management compensation: market salary + bonus + promote (typical PE-style)
- No separate consulting fee (you're the operator)

**Equity / structure mechanics:**
- **Newco equity split:** typically 60–70% you + investor consortium / 25–35% family / 5–10% management option pool
- Standard 8% pref to family's contributed equity (which is their retained stock value), then 80/20 split above pref (split favors you, the operator with promote), then 70/30 above 2× MOIC
- Family-equity put right at year 5 at trailing-12 EBITDA × market multiple
- You-call right at year 7 at same multiple

**Strategy-stack references:**
- Roland **Strategies #21–23 (Sell & Stock Back + Call/Put)** + standard LBO stack

**Success metrics:**
- Acquisition closes within 180 days
- EBITDA ≥ underwriting plan within 24 months
- Family pref returns paid on schedule

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- Family put exercisable year 5 onward
- Your call exercisable year 7 onward
- Either side can trigger an entire-Newco sale process by year 7

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "Why give up control if my family stays in?" → "Because you stop being the operator. You become an investor in your own business. The family keeps the upside but doesn't carry the operating burden. Most family businesses fail in transition because the heirs inherit the operating job; this structure separates ownership from operation while keeping the upside."

### Deal #11 — Refi Bridge with Equity Conversion (Maturing Debt Distress)

**Who it's for:** Owners with maturing 2019–2021 low-coupon debt who can't refinance at today's rates without significant equity injection or operating improvement. Per Radius+ ch. 7, this cohort is sizable: many independents took out 4–5% notes in 2020–2021 that are coming due into a 7–8% market, and Choice Bank's Mikal Christopherson notes "*a lot of groups are hitting pause on acquisitions because they want clarity on rates before they start chasing deals again*."

**The pitch (one line):** "Your loan is maturing. The new rate will crush your DSCR. I bring two things — a capital partner from my network to fill the gap, and the operational lift to support the new debt service. In exchange, I take meaningful equity. You keep the business and you avoid being a forced seller into a buyer's market."

**Deliverables:**
- Refi capital sourcing: private debt funds, regional bank intros, family-office intros, mezz lenders from your network
- Capital-stack structuring (senior debt + mezz + preferred equity + common, optimized for the operator)
- Loan-document negotiation support
- AI-agent operational stack to support the new DSCR
- Optional ongoing operational management

**Fee mechanics:**
- $50K refi success fee at closing (credited back against future equity dilution if equity fully vests)
- 1% capital-sourcing fee on senior debt and mezz arranged
- No monthly retainer otherwise
- Optional ongoing 3–5% revenue management fee if you're running operations post-refi

**Equity mechanics:**
- **10%–20% common equity** depending on capital and operational involvement:
  - 10% for capital sourcing only
  - 15% for capital sourcing + AI-operational stack install
  - 20% for capital sourcing + ops + bringing capital from your own network as participation
- Vesting: 50% at refi close, 50% pro-rata over 36 months
- ROFR, tag, drag

**Strategy-stack references:**
- Roland **Strategies #158/159 (Consolidate / Restructure Debt)** + **#199 (Mezzanine)** + **#182/207/208 (Refinance / New Loan)**

**Success metrics:**
- Refi closes within 120 days of engagement
- New DSCR ≥ 1.30× within 18 months
- Stabilized NOI supports debt service + capex reserve

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- If refi can't close within 180 days: agreement terminates, you're paid only out-of-pocket costs (max $25K)
- If post-refi DSCR drops below 1.10× and stays for 6 months: you have right to install full management or trigger sale process

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "I'd rather sell than give up equity." → "Selling into this market means accepting a 15–25% discount to peak. Giving up 15% equity to keep the asset and ride the recovery — Radius+ data suggests the bottom is forming — is the math-positive choice."
- "Where does your capital come from?" → "Family offices and private debt funds focused on self-storage. I bring intros, not blind pools. You sign with them, not me."

### Deal #12 — ESOP Buyout + Operator Equity (The Tax-Optimized Exit)

**Who it's for:** Owners with significant accumulated gains, an aging workforce they care about, and a desire to exit at a fair price with maximum tax efficiency. Best fit: 15+ year ownership, $20M+ enterprise value, stable workforce of 30+ employees.

**The pitch (one line):** "Your employees buy the company through an ESOP — at a fair, third-party-appraised valuation. You get favorable tax treatment (potentially indefinite deferral via Section 1042 reinvestment). The ESOP needs a professional operator to run the business post-sale — that's where I come in, for an operating role plus equity in the resulting structure."

**Deliverables:**
- ESOP feasibility analysis (working with an ESOP attorney and trustee)
- Independent third-party valuation coordination
- Capital-stack structuring: ESOP loan, seller note, optional outside capital
- Post-sale operating role: CEO or COO of the ESOP company
- Operational improvement plan (full AI-agent stack, revenue management discipline, etc.)
- Ongoing board representation

**Fee mechanics:**
- $50K–$100K structuring fee (paid at deal initiation)
- 1% transaction fee at ESOP close
- Post-close: market CEO/COO salary + bonus

**Equity mechanics:**
- **5%–15% phantom equity or stock option pool** in the ESOP-owned company for management (you + key #2 + key managers)
- Vesting: 5-year cliff or graded vesting per ESOP plan documents
- Liquidity via internal ESOP repurchase obligation or sale event

**Strategy-stack references:**
- Roland **Strategy #213 (ESOP Buyout)**

**Success metrics:**
- ESOP transaction closes within 12 months
- Owner receives proceeds at agreed appraised value
- Section 1042 reinvestment executed if owner elects
- Post-close EBITDA ≥ pre-close baseline within 18 months (ESOPs typically need operating discipline to support debt service)

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- ESOP transaction structure has built-in liquidity mechanisms for employees (annual repurchase obligation funded from cash flow)
- Your equity exits via internal repurchase or future sale event

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "ESOPs sound complicated." → "They are. That's why most owners don't use them — and why those who do extract more value. The tax treatment alone (Section 1042) often produces 20–30% more after-tax proceeds than a straight sale."
- "I don't want my employees running the place." → "They don't. They own the place. The professional operator (me) runs it. The ESOP is an ownership structure, not an operating structure."

### Deal #13 — Roll-Up Platform / HoldCo Wrap (The Multiple-Arbitrage Play)

**Who it's for:** A mid-market operator with 5–15 facilities who wants to grow to 25–50+ via tuck-in acquisitions, but lacks the deal-flow network, institutional polish, or capital-stack sophistication to execute. This is the canonical CFE-into-platform play and is the highest-dollar outcome in the playbook.

**The pitch (one line):** "Your portfolio is a strong base. Wrap it as a professional platform with institutional reporting, centralized revenue management, and a consistent capital stack — then tuck in 10–20 more facilities over 5 years — and the platform commands a different exit multiple than your standalone portfolio does today. I help you build it and I take equity in the wrap, not in the underlying facilities."

**Deliverables:**
- HoldCo formation, structuring, capital-stack architecture
- Consistent operations rollout across all facilities (full AI-agent stack at each)
- Centralized reporting, banking, treasury, insurance
- Centralized revenue management (the Radius+-cited *"yield management systems capable of analyzing local conditions, seasonality, competitor pricing, and demand profiles in real time"*)
- Group purchasing
- Off-market deal sourcing pipeline (cold outreach, distressed/maturing-debt lists, broker network, owner-direct)
- Underwriting model + BOV for every shortlisted deal
- Institutional-buyer relationship building (PE, family offices, REIT M&A teams)
- Year-3 to year-5 exit positioning (QoE, deal book, banker selection)

**Fee mechanics:**
- $25K/mo HoldCo management retainer
- 1.5% acquisition fee on any HoldCo tuck-in acquisition
- 2% disposition fee on HoldCo exit (capped, split if co-broker)
- Optional capital-raise fees if you're sourcing institutional capital for the platform

**Equity / structure mechanics:**
- **10%–15% of HoldCo common equity** (not underlying facility-level equity)
- Promote structure: 8% pref to operator's contributed equity, then 80/20 above pref to 2× MOIC, then 70/30 thereafter
- Operator (the original owner) contributes facilities at agreed-fair value into HoldCo
- 5-year minimum hold; year-5 sale process default

**Strategy-stack references:**
- Roland **Strategy #206 (Roll-Up)** + **#126/127 (SPV)** + **#122 (PPM)** for tuck-in equity tranches

**Success metrics:**
- HoldCo NOI ≥ 25% above sum-of-facilities baseline within 36 months
- Cap-rate compression on exit ≥ 50 bps vs standalone (typical for institutional platform vs single-facility — on $5M of NOI that's $5–10M of additional sale value)
- At least 2 add-on acquisitions closed per year of engagement

**Exit / failure mechanics:**
- Year-5 sale process default unless both parties extend
- Failed exit at year 5: operator buyout right at appraisal, less owed promote
- Your call right at year 7 at appraisal

**Realistic objections + responses:**
- "I don't need a HoldCo — my facilities run fine." → "Today they do. But five facilities sold separately get retail buyers at retail cap rates. A five-facility platform with institutional reporting, centralized revenue management, and a proven roll-up engine gets institutional buyers at institutional cap rates. The Radius+ 2026 data is explicit: scaled operators command meaningfully better economics. That's typically 50–100 bps of cap-rate compression on exit, which on $5M of NOI is $7M–$15M of additional sale value."
- "What if your acquisitions hurt my existing facilities?" → "Every acquisition has its own underwriting and is approved by both of us. Bilateral veto. No forced add-ons."

## 8. Filters Before You Engage

Before spending a single hour on a mid-market CFE pursuit, run the **People / Market / Fun (PMF)** filter and add three institutional filters:

- **People** — Is the owner coachable? Do they actually want to grow, or do they want validation? Does the board/family align?
- **Market** — Is the sector tailwind or headwind in their MSAs? Use Radius+ market-level data. Self-storage 2026–2027 is mixed; pick markets carefully — Radius+ ch. 9 highlights wide MSA-level dispersion.
- **Fun** — Will you enjoy 24+ months inside this business?
- **Capacity** — Is there real headroom for growth (achieved-rate vs web-rate gap, low insurance attach, low ECRI discipline, weak captured-call rate), or is the business already operating near its ceiling?
- **Transferability** — Will the value you create survive a change of owner, or does it walk out the door with the founder?
- **Financeability** — Will senior lenders and PPM investors touch this business in 2 years? If not, your option is a paper tiger.

## 9. Common Failure Modes at This Scale

- **Underpricing the consult.** A $5K/month retainer screams junior to a $20M-EBITDA owner. Use $25K–$60K/month or quarterly six-figure structures.
- **Skipping the pre-nup.** When you're 18 months in and the relationship sours, you need pre-negotiated buyout terms. Always.
- **Vesting that's too back-loaded.** Roland's rule: never less than 20% up front. At this scale, push for 50% or Instant Earn-In.
- **Option strike priced off a moving target.** Lock it as a fixed number or a fixed formula (e.g., "6.5× trailing 12-month EBITDA as of execution date") — never "fair market value at exercise."
- **No SPV / Newco.** Personal liability on a $30M LBO is unacceptable. Always Newco.
- **Forgetting the real estate.** On any asset-heavy business — self-storage especially — the OpCo/PropCo split is where most of the financial-engineering value is hiding. Always model it.
- **Mis-pegging working capital.** At this scale the WC peg is a real number. Get an accountant on it at LOI, not at close.
- **Ignoring family dynamics.** In family-owned deals the family is the deal. A board fight between siblings in year 2 can kill an otherwise good deal.
- **Pitching tech to operators.** Same rule as `CFE-DEAL-IDEAS.md`: never lead with "AI" or "agents" — pitch the operator outcomes (missed calls captured, ECRI discipline, insurance attach, NOI lift). Sophisticated owners are slightly more tolerant of tech jargon than mom-and-pops, but the lift-in-dollars frame still wins.

## 10. Quick-Reference Checklist

When you identify a target large-independent business, work this list:

- [ ] Pass PMF + Capacity + Transferability + Financeability filters
- [ ] Quote your diagnostic fee at market ($50K–$150K) and deliver a written Value Creation Plan
- [ ] Convert to F2E (Deal #3) with monthly retainer + equity + Greater-Of clause
- [ ] Embed Full-Option or Partial-Option to Acquire with fixed strike, 2-year window, kill fee
- [ ] Negotiate Instant or Temporal Earn-In with acceleration on termination
- [ ] Set up Newco/SPV before any equity transfer
- [ ] Pre-negotiate business pre-nup (buyout formula + put/call mechanics)
- [ ] Model OpCo/PropCo split on day 30; identify SLB upside
- [ ] Identify achieved-rate-vs-web-rate gap; close it within first 6 months
- [ ] Install portfolio-wide AI-agent stack (capture, ECRI, insurance attach, delinquency) in first 90 days
- [ ] Begin assembling deal-funding stack (carryback + RE SLB + PPM + senior + mezz) by month 12
- [ ] Begin Quality-of-Earnings prep by month 15
- [ ] Exercise option month 18–24, OR execute LBO structure, OR position for HoldCo exit at year 5

## 11. 2026 Market Context — Why This Playbook Is Timely

The Radius+ 2026 Forecast data strongly validates the CFE approach for large independents right now:

**The bid-ask spread is the defining problem of 2026.** Tyler Lambert (Live Oak Bank): *"We are seeing deals fall apart because buyers cannot bridge the gap between seller pricing expectations and today's debt costs."* Michael Mazur (Newcoast): *"Everyone is waiting for the reset. Sellers still want peak pricing, and buyers cannot get there with current debt."* And: *"It is not that interest in acquisitions is gone. It is that nothing pencils unless values adjust."* This is precisely the gap the CFE Evil Plan is designed to bridge — seller gets their number, buyer doesn't overpay because the option price is locked before the value is created.

**Acquisition volume has collapsed.** Per Radius+ ch. 7, transaction activity peaked in 2021 and fell to roughly half that level by 2024, with only a modest rebound in 2025. This means:
- Less competition from institutional buyers for mid-market targets
- More owners who wanted to sell in 2022–2023 and couldn't — they're increasingly fatigued
- Option prices locked today reflect depressed valuations, not peak pricing

**The fragmentation math is in your favor.** The US self-storage industry has approximately 43,500 small private companies, 6,500 large private companies, and 9,000 public-company facilities. The largest public REITs control only a fraction of facilities. Radius+ ch. 8 explicitly cites *"more sophisticated operations lead to more stable pricing,"* *"stronger property-level performance,"* and *"improved legitimacy and capital access"* as benefits of scale — meaning a platform of 15–25 independently acquired facilities still commands a meaningful premium over single-asset pricing at exit.

**Third-party management is the CFE wedge.** Many independent operators who won't sell are turning to third-party management to professionalize operations. Dave Deems (Florida SSA / Storage Asset Management): *"A lot of smaller owners are realizing they don't have the systems or scale to operate as efficiently in this environment."* That's a consulting opportunity for Deal #1 → Deal #3 → Deal #5. Per Radius+ ch. 8, the top 10 third-party managers collectively manage ~370,000 facilities of square-footage equivalent — the third-party management channel is the canonical entry point for a CFE relationship at scale.

**The interest-rate environment favors patient structured deals.** Senior debt is expensive. Cash buyers are scarce. Sellers who need to exit can't get their price from a cash buyer — but a structured deal (seller carry + equity + option + RE SLB) can get them their price over time. You're the only buyer who can say yes at their number. Radius+ ch. 9: *"As long as interest rates remain elevated and debt remains expensive, the bid–ask spread is expected to persist, limiting deal volume and keeping many potential transactions on hold. Buyers will continue to underwrite conservatively, prioritizing markets with limited new supply, proven demand durability, and assets that can support higher equity contributions."*

**The supply pipeline is collapsing — long-term tailwind.** Per Radius+ ch. 5, a 70% decline in self-storage deliveries is expected in 2026 from the 2023 peak. Stan Bonilla (Safeguard) called it the *"toughest development environment I have ever witnessed."* Bill Hobin (William Warren Group) echoed. This means: facilities you acquire now, in markets where the supply pipeline is thinning, will benefit disproportionately when demand recovers — which is the patient, structured-deal thesis.

## 12. Open Questions to Resolve Before Going to Market

1. What's your **portfolio strategy**: deep equity in 2–3 platforms (Deals #5, #6, #13, #14) or wide equity in 8–15 smaller engagements (Deals #1–#4)?
2. What's your **outside-capital network**: family offices, PPM investors, mezz funds, RE SLB buyers — what's your committed-but-not-syndicated pipeline?
3. What's your **first-loss capital budget** for Deals #11 (Refi Bridge) and #14 (LBO) where capital is implied?
4. What's your **legal template stack** for Deal #5 (F2E + Option), Deal #8 (RE SLB), and Deal #14 (LBO)? Roland's templates need self-storage-specific addenda (gate code transitions, lien-law/auction-law variance by state, tenant-data DPA, PCI compliance).
5. What's your **QoE firm bench**? At this scale you'll need 2–3 independent QoE firms on standby for both diagnostic and pre-close phases.
6. What's your **first 5 targets list**? Use Radius+ for owner identification (large private operators with 10+ facilities in tailwind MSAs), then cross-reference with broker network and maturing-debt lists.
7. Do you have a **first case study** at scale — a Deal #3 or #5 with audited results — before pitching Deal #13 or #14 to a sophisticated owner who will ask?
