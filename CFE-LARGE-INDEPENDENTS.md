# Roland Frasier CFE — Deals for Large Independent Businesses (Non-REIT, Non-Mom-and-Pop)

Source: Synthesis of Roland Frasier EPIC Network materials — applied to the mid-market segment that sits between a single-owner operator and an institutional REIT. For self-storage this is the regional/independent operator: ~5–50 facilities, professional staff, formal financials, often a CFO and a board or family ownership group, EBITDA roughly $2M–$30M.

Why this segment is different:
- Owners are sophisticated. They've already been pitched. Cold "wallet vs. pipe wrench" gambits fall flat.
- Books are clean (or close). You'll see audited or reviewed financials, real working-capital schedules, real DD.
- There is almost always a moat-shaped gap: ownership is tired or undercapitalized vs. opportunity, but the business is too big to be cheap and too small to attract REIT bids.
- This is exactly where Roland's *Consulting → Equity → Option to Acquire* playbook is most effective.

---

## 1. Target Profile — Who Fits This Playbook

A large independent business worth approaching with CFE-style structures typically shows:

- **Owner-operator fatigue at scale** — founder is still CEO, business has outgrown their operating model
- **Stalled growth despite a strong base** — revenue flat or single-digit growth for 2–3 years
- **Sub-institutional sophistication** — no PE owner, no banker on retainer
- **Capital constraint** — refuses to take outside money, can't fund the next expansion themselves
- **Succession ambiguity** — no clear internal successor; kids aren't taking it; key #2 is restless
- **Underexploited dormant assets** — customer list, real estate, brand, supplier relationships, data, unused capacity, geographic footprint
- **Exit interest within 2–5 years** but no plan for how

For self-storage specifically: regional operators with 10–40 facilities, mixed owned/leased, often holding company structure, frequently family-owned 2nd generation. They will *not* sell at REIT cap rates to you on a cash deal — but they will entertain a structured deal that protects the price they want.

---

## 2. Why CFE Beats a Cash Offer at This Level

Mid-market independent owners reject lowball cash bids on reflex. CFE flips the conversation:

1. **You're not negotiating price first** — you're solving their growth problem.
2. **You're paid to learn the business** before you commit capital.
3. **You lock optionality** (purchase option) at today's price *before* you create tomorrow's value.
4. **Equity bridges the gap** between what they want and what the market will pay.

The Roland framing: *"Exit outcomes are usually decided long before a deal is on the table."* For larger sellers, you are positioning to be the only credible buyer at the seller's number, two years before they go to market.

## 3. The Core Sequence — "Consulting Into Equity Into Acquisition"

Adapted from Roland's "Evil Plan." At mid-market scale, each step is more formal:

### Step 1 — Diagnostic Engagement (Paid)
- **Not free** at this level. Free advice signals you're junior. Charge.
- Roland's rates as anchor: **$18K–$20K half-day virtual**, **$50K/quarter** advisory, **$150K/6 months** half-day monthly.
- The diagnostic produces a written **Value Creation Plan**: 3–5 specific initiatives that close the gap between today's value and the seller's price target.
- Output is *theirs to keep* — they're free to walk and run the plan themselves. Almost no mid-market owner does.

### Step 2 — Engagement to Execute (Fee + Equity)
- Convert to a **Fee-to-Equity (F2E)** structure: monthly retainer plus equity component.
- Equity range at this scale: **5%–20%** for active operating involvement (Roland's Type 2: Operating Deal), or **0.25%–5%** for advisory-only (Type 1).
- Use **"Greater Of"** language: monthly cash floor *or* % of distributions, whichever is greater. Protects you in slow quarters.
- Equity vesting: **Instant Earn-In** is the ask. Fall back to **Temporal Milestone** (at least 20% up front, no less than 50%, rest vesting over time, full acceleration on termination without cause).
- Always include the **business pre-nup**: pre-negotiated buyout formula (e.g., "40% of 4× 12-month trailing SDE paid over 18 months") so divorce terms are settled while you're friends.

### Step 3 — Lock the Purchase Option
- **Full-Option to Acquire** (Strategy #132) or **Partial-Option** (#131) embedded in the same agreement.
- Two-year option window standard. Three years if they're hesitant.
- **Price is fixed today** — before the value you're about to create gets baked in.
- Frame it as alignment: *"If I'm going to put my reputation and time into this, I need the option to buy you out within two years. If the plan doesn't work, the option expires worthless and you've lost nothing."*

### Step 4 — Create the Value
- Execute the plan. Roland's expectation: 12–24 months of measurable growth before exercising.
- Throughout, you're inside the books — best possible DD environment.
- This is also where you stack the deal-funding strategies below to make the eventual close cash-light.

### Step 5 — Exercise the Option, Stack Strategies to Fund the Close
- Exercise at the locked-in price.
- The business is worth materially more than the strike price. The delta is your equity-financed "discount."
- Now apply the **216 Deal Funding Strategies** to fund the actual purchase with as little personal cash as possible (see Section 5).

## 4. CFE Deal Types at Mid-Market Scale

From Roland's typology, adjusted for who actually sits across the table:

| Type | When It Fits | Typical Equity | Notes |
|------|--------------|----------------|-------|
| **Advisory** | Owner wants outside perspective, board seat, network access. Family business, generational transition. | 0.25%–5% | Board seat, 3–4 meetings/year. Often the wedge into a larger deal later. |
| **Operating** | Owner is tired, growth has stalled, they need an executor. | 5%–20% (cap higher for true co-CEO arrangements) | This is the most common "Evil Plan" path. |
| **Transactional** | They're raising capital, doing a tuck-in acquisition, or prepping for exit. | 1%–20% + cash | Pay structure mirrors a banker fee; equity is the alignment piece. |
| **Performance** | Specific outcome — e.g., open 5 new locations, launch a new line of business, integrate an acquisition. | 5%–25% on the segment | Use **Performance Segment Earn-In** so failure in other parts of the business doesn't poison your stake. |

## 5. Deal-Funding Strategies That Actually Work at This Scale

Out of Roland's 216, these are the ones that punch above their weight when the target is a $20M–$200M enterprise-value independent business. Mom-and-pop tricks (Card Cash Advance, IRA ROBS, HELOC, SBA Microloan) don't scale here — these do:

### Pre-Closing (used to structure the offer)

- **#1 Asset Carve-Out** — At this scale, working capital, real estate, and non-core subsidiaries are large enough to materially reduce the headline price. Always carve.
- **#119 Leveraged Buyout (LBO)** — The base structure. Senior debt secured by the target's assets and cash flow.
- **#120 Management Buyout (MBO)** — Existing #2 wants to be #1. You partner with them and bring the capital structure.
- **#122 Private Placement Memorandum (PPM)** — Raise the equity tranche from accredited investors via Reg D. Mid-market deals routinely raise $2M–$20M this way.
- **#47 Staggered Tranches PPM** — Raise the equity in stages tied to deal milestones; reduces the dilution of front-loading.
- **#126/127 SPV Asset/Equity Merger** — Newco isolates liabilities and lets you bring in co-investors per deal.
- **#128/129 Partial Acquisition (one-step or two-step)** — Buy 51% now, buy the rest on a put/call schedule. Especially powerful when seller wants to stay involved.
- **#82–87 Seller Carryback (the OC ladder)** — Negotiate from "no note, no interest, balloon" down through the five fallback positions. On a $30M deal, even a 30% carry at 5% saves you ~$9M of senior debt capacity.
- **#138 Earn-Out** — Bridge a valuation gap by paying part of the price out of future EBITDA.
- **#21–23 Sell & Stock Back + Call/Put** — Seller sells to your Newco, takes back minority stock, you have call (or they have put). Common in family-owned exits where heirs want to stay in.
- **#199 Mezzanine Financing** — Subordinated debt fills the gap between senior debt and equity. Standard on $20M+ deals.
- **#148 Real Estate Sale & Leaseback (RE SLB)** — For self-storage especially: split the OpCo from the PropCo, sell the real estate to a long-hold investor at a low cap rate, lease it back, use proceeds to fund the OpCo acquisition. *This is the single highest-leverage structure for self-storage independents.*
- **#107 Real Estate Split-Off** — Same idea, kept inside the seller's family — they keep the dirt, you buy the operating business.
- **#206 Roll-Up** — Use the first acquisition as a platform; subsequent tuck-ins inherit the platform's debt capacity and multiple arbitrage.
- **#213 ESOP Buyout** — Seller sells to an Employee Stock Ownership Plan at favorable tax treatment; you structure and run the resulting company for equity + management fee.

### Post-Closing (used to make the deal cash-flow positive faster)

- **#158/159 Consolidate / Restructure Debt** — Refinance the seller's existing facilities at better terms immediately post-close.
- **#182/207/208 Real Estate Refinance / New Loan** — Cash-out refi any owned real estate post-close to return capital to investors.
- **#162 FF&E Sale-Leaseback** — Equipment, vehicles, tech stack.
- **#214 Revenue-Based Financing** — Non-dilutive growth capital for the platform.
- **#192 Apply Target's Line of Credit to Acquisition Funding** — Their unused LOC is your closing capital.

### Specifically for Self-Storage Independent Operators
- Stack **RE SLB (#148)** + **Seller Carryback (#82–87)** + **PPM (#122)** + **Mezzanine (#199)**. This is the canonical structure: real estate buyer pays ~60% of the price, seller carries ~15%, PPM raises ~15%, mezz fills ~10%, your equity contribution can approach zero with management promote.
- Use **Working Capital Adjustment (#64)** carefully — at this scale the WC peg is a real number, and getting it wrong costs millions at close.

## 6. Operational Value You Deliver in the CFE Period

This is what the seller is actually buying from you during the engagement (from `consulting-for-equity-deal-points.md`). At mid-market scale these are the levers that move enterprise value most:

- **Market expansion strategy** and in-market introductions
- **Conversion / revenue-per-customer testing** (price ladder work, upsell engineering)
- **Strategic partnership negotiation** and integration deals
- **Growth-via-acquisition strategy formulation** — often more valuable than the consulting itself, because it teaches the seller what *their* business is worth in a roll-up
- **Centers-of-influence introductions** within your network
- **Exit preparation** — quality of earnings, normalized EBITDA add-backs, deck, banker selection
- **Negotiation of major deals** the company enters into (lease renewals, vendor consolidation, large customer contracts)
- **Dormant asset activation** — the customer list isn't being marketed to, the unused acreage isn't being developed, the brand isn't licensed, the data isn't being sold. Find these. Activate them. The lift is fast and unambiguous.

## 7. The Three Bites of the Apple — At Scale

Roland's "3 Bites" model holds at mid-market with bigger absolute numbers:

1. **Consult** — $50K–$300K in cash fees during the engagement
2. **Profit distributions** — 5%–20% of distributions during your equity-hold period
3. **Exit** — equity payout when you exercise the option and resell the platform, OR when the platform is recapitalized and you take chips off the table

Stack a **Meta Deal**: get a stake in future acquisitions the platform does because you sourced the first one. On a roll-up, this is where the real money is.

## 8. Filters Before You Engage

Before spending a single hour on a mid-market CFE pursuit, run the **People / Market / Fun (PFM)** filter and add three institutional filters:

- **People** — Is the owner coachable? Do they actually want to grow, or do they want validation?
- **Market** — Is the sector tailwind or headwind? Self-storage 2026–2027 is mixed; pick markets carefully.
- **Fun** — Will you enjoy 24 months inside this business?
- **Capacity** — Is there real headroom for growth, or is this business already saturated?
- **Transferability** — Will the value you create survive a change of owner, or does it walk out the door with the founder?
- **Financeability** — Will senior lenders touch this business in two years? If not, your option is a paper tiger.

---

## 9. Common Failure Modes at This Scale

- **Underpricing the consult.** A $5K/month retainer screams junior to a $20M-revenue owner. Use $25K–$50K/month or quarterly six-figure structures.
- **Skipping the pre-nup.** When you're 18 months in and the relationship sours, you need pre-negotiated buyout terms. Always.
- **Vesting that's too back-loaded.** Roland's rule: never less than 20% up front. At this scale, push for 50%.
- **Option strike priced off a moving target.** Lock it as a fixed number or a fixed formula (e.g., "5.5× trailing 12-month EBITDA as of execution date") — never "fair market value at exercise."
- **No SPV.** Personal liability on a $30M LBO is unacceptable. Always Newco.
- **Forgetting the real estate.** On any asset-heavy business — self-storage especially — the OpCo/PropCo split is where most of the financial engineering value is hiding.

## 10. Quick-Reference Checklist

When you identify a target large independent business, work this list:

- [ ] Pass PFM + Capacity + Transferability + Financeability filters
- [ ] Quote your diagnostic fee at market ($25K+ at minimum)
- [ ] Deliver Value Creation Plan as written document seller can keep
- [ ] Propose F2E engagement: monthly fee + equity + Greater-Of clause
- [ ] Embed Full-Option to Acquire with fixed strike, 2-year window
- [ ] Negotiate Instant or Temporal Earn-In with acceleration on termination
- [ ] Set up SPV before any equity transfer
- [ ] Pre-negotiate business pre-nup (buyout formula + put/call mechanics)
- [ ] Identify Dormant Assets in first 30 days; activate in first 90
- [ ] Begin assembling deal-funding stack (carryback + RE SLB + PPM + senior + mezz) by month 12
- [ ] Begin Quality-of-Earnings prep by month 18
- [ ] Exercise option month 18–24

## 11. 2026 Market Context — Why This Playbook Is Timely (Radius+ 2026 Report)

The Radius+ 2026 Forecast data strongly validates the CFE approach for large independents right now:

**The bid-ask spread is the defining problem of 2026.** As Tyler Lambert of Live Oak Bank stated directly: *"We are seeing deals fall apart because buyers cannot bridge the gap between seller pricing expectations and today's debt costs."* Michael Mazur echoed: *"It is not that interest in acquisitions is gone. It is that nothing pencils unless values adjust."* This is precisely the gap the CFE Evil Plan is designed to bridge — seller gets their number, buyer doesn't overpay by locking the option price before creating the value.

**Acquisition volume has collapsed.** Transaction activity peaked in 2021 and fell to roughly half that level by 2024, with only a modest rebound in 2025. This means:
- Less competition from institutional buyers for mid-market targets
- More owners who wanted to sell in 2022–2023 and couldn't — they're increasingly fatigued
- Option prices locked today reflect depressed valuations, not peak pricing

**The fragmentation math is in your favor.** The US self-storage industry has approximately 43,500 small private companies and 9,000 mid-size private companies. The largest public REITs control only a fraction of facilities. Scale advantages are real — larger operators demonstrate stronger pricing discipline, higher occupancy, and better capital access. This means a platform of 10–20 independently acquired facilities still commands a meaningful premium over single-asset pricing at exit.

**Third-party management is the CFE wedge.** Many independent operators who won't sell are turning to third-party management to professionalize operations. This is the ideal initial relationship — you're inside the business, you understand the books, you've proven value, and you can propose a CFE engagement before a competitor does. As Dave Deems noted: *"A lot of smaller owners are realizing they don't have the systems or scale to operate as efficiently in this environment."* That's a consulting opportunity.

**The interest rate environment favors patient structured deals.** Senior debt is expensive. Cash buyers are scarce. Sellers who need to exit can't get their price from a cash buyer — but a structured deal (seller carry + equity + option) can get them their price over time. You're the only buyer who can say yes at their number.
